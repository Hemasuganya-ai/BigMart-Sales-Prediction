## Dataset description
`train_bigmart.csv` is the **BigMart Sales** dataset: 8,523 rows, one row per product listed at a specific outlet, with 12 original columns covering item attributes (weight, fat content, visibility on shelf, type, MRP/price) and outlet attributes (identifier, establishment year, size, location tier, store format), plus the target **`Item_Outlet_Sales`** — the total sales generated by that item at that outlet. This is a supervised regression setup: the eventual modeling goal (Part 2) is predicting `Item_Outlet_Sales` from item and outlet characteristics.

One numeric feature was engineered: **`Outlet_Age`** = 2013 − `Outlet_Establishment_Year`. The raw establishment year (1985–2009) is a calendar label, not a meaningful quantity on its own; converting it to "how many years the outlet has been operating (as of the 2013 data-collection date)" makes it an interpretable, model-ready numeric feature and is what allows a fair comparison against the other numeric columns in the skewness/correlation tasks.

## Null value analysis

| Column | Nulls | % |
|---|---|---|
| Item_Weight | 1,463 | 17.17% |
| Outlet_Size | 2,410 | 28.28% |

**`Outlet_Size` exceeds the 20% threshold.** Per the task rules, it is not median-filled (it's categorical anyway, not numeric), and dropping 28% of rows was rejected as too costly. Instead, its missingness looks structural rather than random — specific outlets (mostly Grocery Stores and some Supermarket Type1s) simply never reported a size — so it was filled with the **mode of `Outlet_Size` within each `Outlet_Type`** (e.g., Grocery Store → "Small", Supermarket Type3 → "Medium"), which is more defensible than a single global mode.

**`Item_Weight`** (17.2%, below the threshold) was filled with the column **median**, not the mean. **Why median:** item weight is a physical product attribute with a wide range (4.5–21.4 kg-equivalent units) driven by very different product categories (e.g., snack packets vs. household items) — heavier/lighter niche products can pull a mean away from what a "typical" item actually weighs, and the median is more robust to that category-driven spread. This same median-over-mean logic is revisited more rigorously for the two most-skewed columns in Task 7a.

## Duplicate detection
`df.duplicated()` finds **0** duplicate rows across the full dataset — every row is a unique item–outlet combination, so no rows were removed and no null percentages changed.

## Data type correction
- **`Item_Fat_Content`** had five raw values that really represent only two categories, due to inconsistent data entry: `'LF'`, `'Low Fat'`, `'low fat'` (→ **Low Fat**) and `'Regular'`, `'reg'` (→ **Regular**). These were standardized with `.replace()` before casting to `category` — this is the "incorrect inferred representation" fix, since the raw column looked like 5 categories but is really 2.
- Six repetitive string columns (`Item_Fat_Content`, `Item_Type`, `Outlet_Identifier`, `Outlet_Size`, `Outlet_Location_Type`, `Outlet_Type`) were converted to `category` dtype.
- **Memory usage:** 3,609.15 KB → 901.18 KB, a **75.0% reduction** — a very large drop because nearly every non-numeric column in this dataset is low-cardinality and repetitive (only 10 outlets, 16 item types, etc.), which is exactly what `category` dtype is built to compress.

## Descriptive statistics & skewness
`df.describe()` is in the notebook. Skewness per numeric column:

| Column | Skewness |
|---|---|
| Item_Weight | +0.08 |
| **Item_Visibility** | **+1.17** |
| Item_MRP | +0.13 |
| Outlet_Age | +0.40 |
| **Item_Outlet_Sales** | **+1.18** |

**`Item_Outlet_Sales` has the highest absolute skewness (+1.18) — positively skewed** (with `Item_Visibility` at +1.17 an extremely close second, see Task 7a). A positive skew means a long right tail: most item-outlet combinations generate modest sales, but a meaningful minority of high-performing product/outlet pairs pull the distribution far to the right (max ≈ $13,087 vs. a median of ≈ $1,794). **Consequence for mean imputation:** the mean ($2,181) sits noticeably above the median ($1,794) here, so filling missing Sales values with the mean would overstate what a "typical" item-outlet actually sells — the median is the safer, more representative fill value.

## Outlier detection (IQR method)

| Column | Q1 | Q3 | IQR | Lower bound | Upper bound | Outliers |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Item_Outlet_Sales | 834.25 | 3,101.30 | 2,267.05 | −2,566.33 | 6,501.87 | **186 rows (2.18%)** |
| Item_Visibility | 0.0270 | 0.0946 | 0.0676 | −0.0744 | 0.1960 | **144 rows (1.69%)** |

- **Item_Outlet_Sales**: 2.18% of rows sit above the $6,501.87 upper bound (no lower-bound issue, sales can't be negative). These are genuinely high-performing item/outlet combinations — large Supermarket Type3 stores selling premium items — not data-entry errors.
- **Item_Visibility**: 1.69% of rows exceed the upper bound of ~0.196 (visibility is a proportion of shelf space, so very high values represent unusually prominent product placement). Note separately: **526 rows have `Item_Visibility` exactly equal to 0**, which is physically implausible for a stocked item and looks like a data-entry placeholder rather than a true zero — worth flagging for Part 2 even though it isn't formally an IQR outlier on the low end.
- **Handling decision for Part 2:** these outliers will **not** be dropped. They represent real business variation (genuinely strong sellers, prominently displayed items) that a sales-prediction model needs to learn from. The plan is to **cap (winsorize) Item_Outlet_Sales at the upper IQR bound only for models sensitive to extreme values** (e.g., linear/ridge regression), while leaving raw values intact for tree-based models (Random Forest, Gradient Boosting), which handle outliers natively. The `Item_Visibility == 0` rows will be treated as a missing-value pattern to impute (e.g., with the item's category-level median visibility) rather than as valid measurements.

## Correlation heat map & causality discussion
The **highest absolute Pearson correlation is `Item_MRP` ↔ `Item_Outlet_Sales` at r ≈ 0.568** — a moderate positive correlation, the strongest relationship among all numeric columns by a wide margin (next-highest is `Item_Visibility` ↔ `Item_Outlet_Sales` at only −0.13). This is intuitive on its face (higher-priced items generate more sales revenue per unit sold), but it's worth being careful about causality: **MRP mechanically contributes to the Sales figure** (Sales here is a dollar total, and price is one of its multiplicative components — Sales ≈ price × units sold), so part of this correlation is close to definitional rather than a discovered causal insight. A plausible **alternative/confounding explanation** is `Item_Type` or `Outlet_Type`: certain product categories (e.g., electronics/household vs. snacks) are both priced higher **and** stocked preferentially at larger-format stores (Supermarket Type3) that also sell more units — so category and store format could be driving both MRP and Sales simultaneously rather than MRP causing higher sales on its own.

## Task 7a — Imputation strategy comparison (mean vs. median)
The two numeric columns with the highest absolute skewness are **Item_Outlet_Sales** (skew ≈ +1.18) and **Item_Visibility** (skew ≈ +1.17) — remarkably close to each other:

| Column | Mean (before imputation) | Median (before imputation) | Skew |
|---|---|---|---|
| Item_Outlet_Sales | 2,181.29 | 1,794.33 | +1.18 |
| Item_Visibility | 0.0661 | 0.0539 | +1.17 |

Neither column had missing values at this stage (this dataset's nulls were confined to `Item_Weight` and `Outlet_Size`, both already handled in Tasks 2 and 5), so `fillna()` here is a confirmatory no-op — but the reasoning still applies and would govern any future nulls: both columns are **positively skewed**, so in both cases the mean is pulled *upward* by a minority of very large values (big sales totals; unusually prominent shelf placements) — the **median** is the more representative statistic for imputation in both. `isnull().sum()` confirms **0** nulls remain in both columns.

## Task 7b — Spearman vs. Pearson
Spearman and Pearson matrices are both printed in the notebook. The three column pairs ranked by |Spearman − Pearson|:

| Pair | \|Spearman − Pearson\| | Spearman | Pearson |
|---|---|---|---|
| Outlet_Age ↔ Item_Outlet_Sales | 0.0921 | **−0.0429** | **+0.0491** |
| Outlet_Age ↔ Item_Visibility | 0.0199 | 0.0549 | 0.0748 |
| Outlet_Age ↔ Item_Weight | 0.0137 | 0.0060 | −0.0077 |

- **Outlet_Age ↔ Item_Outlet_Sales** is the standout: the two measures don't just differ in magnitude, they **flip sign** (Pearson slightly positive, Spearman slightly negative). Both are small in absolute terms, but this flip signals that the age/sales relationship is **not well captured by either a straight line or a clean monotonic rank pattern** — most likely something non-monotonic is happening (e.g., mid-age outlets outperforming both the newest and the very oldest ones), which neither correlation measure alone would characterize correctly. This is a case for visual inspection (grouped bar/box plot by outlet, done in Task 7c) rather than trusting either correlation number in isolation.
- **Outlet_Age ↔ Item_Visibility**: |Spearman| (0.055) < |Pearson| (0.075) here, so if anything the relationship looks *slightly more linear than monotonic* — but both values are small enough that this pair carries little practical signal either way.
- **Outlet_Age ↔ Item_Weight**: both correlations are essentially zero (−0.01 to +0.01) — no relationship, linear or monotonic, between an outlet's age and the weight of items it stocks, which is expected since these are unrelated attributes.
- **For Part 2 feature selection**, **Pearson will be the primary reference** for the strong, genuinely useful signal in this dataset (`Item_MRP` ↔ `Item_Outlet_Sales`, r ≈ 0.57), since that relationship is close to linear. For `Outlet_Age`, given the sign flip, neither correlation measure alone is trustworthy — it should be encoded as a **categorical/binned feature** (e.g., "outlet age bucket") for Part 2 rather than trusted as a clean linear numeric predictor.

## Task 7c — Grouped aggregation (Item_Outlet_Sales by Outlet_Type)

| Outlet_Type | mean | std | count |
|---|---|---|---|
| Grocery Store | 339.83 | 260.85 | 1,083 |
| Supermarket Type1 | 2,316.18 | 1,515.97 | 5,577 |
| Supermarket Type2 | 1,995.50 | 1,375.93 | 928 |
| **Supermarket Type3** | **3,694.04** | **2,127.76** | 935 |

- **Highest mean:** Supermarket Type3 ($3,694.04). **Highest std:** Supermarket Type3 ($2,127.76) — the same group.
- **Is high within-group std a concern?** Yes, to a real but limited degree. Supermarket Type3's standard deviation (2,127.76) is more than half its own mean, meaning individual item-level sales within that store format still vary substantially — knowing "this is a Supermarket Type3 item" narrows the expected sales range considerably (it rules out the very low Grocery Store range) but does **not** pin down the value precisely; other features (Item_MRP, Item_Type, Item_Visibility) will still be needed to explain the remaining within-group spread.
- **Ratio of highest to lowest group mean:** 3,694.04 / 339.83 = **10.87**. A ratio this large (nearly 11×) indicates `Outlet_Type` carries **strong predictive signal** — average sales differ dramatically across store formats, making this one of the most useful categorical features in the dataset for Part 2 modeling.
